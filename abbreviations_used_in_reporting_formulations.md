**Appendix 1: Abbreviations Used in Reporting Formulations**

When reporting formulations, three common abbreviations are often used:

* *v/v* (volume of solute per volume of solution)  
* *w/v* (weight of solute per volume of solution)  
* *w/w* (weight of solute per weight of solution)

***v/v*** **(volume of solute per volume of solution)**

In this case, the solute of interest is a liquid present in a liquid solution. Crucially, the denominator is the total volume of the solution, not just the solvent:

% (*v/v*) \= ((volume of solute) / (volume of solution)) \* 100

For example, “Hydrochloric Acid, 10% (*v/v*), Aqueous Solution” could be made by taking 10 mL of hydrochloric acid (HCl) and diluting it with 90 mL of water, to make up a total volume of 100 mL. The amount of HCl in the final solution is thus:

 (10 mL HCl / 100 mL solution) \* 100 \= 10% (*v/v*)

***w/v*** **(weight of solute per volume of solution)**

This weight per volume representation, also known as *m/v* (mass per volume) is typically used when a solid is dissolved in a liquid. As before, the volume of solution used is the total volume with the solute present in solution.

% (w/v) \= ((mass of solute in grams) / (volume of solution in mL)) \* 100

For example, “Sodium chloride 10% (w/v) in aqueous solution” could be made by taking 10 g of sodium chloride and adding sufficient water to produce 100 mL of solution. Crucially, that is not the same as adding 100 mL of water. The amount of NaCl in the final solution is thus:

(10 g NaCl / 100 mL solution) \* 100 \= 10% (*w/v*)

***w/w*** **(weight of solute per weight of solution)**

This mass per mass unit ignores the volumes of the solute and solvents:

% (w/w) \= ((mass of solute) / (mass of solution)) \* 100

For example, “Hydrochloric Acid, 10% (w/w), Aqueous Solution” could be made by taking 10 g of hydrochloric acid (HCl) and diluting it with 90 g of water to make up a solution with a total mass of 100 g.

(10 g HCl / 100 g solution) \* 100 \= 10% (*w/w*)
